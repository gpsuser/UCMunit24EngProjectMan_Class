## Introduction: Making Sense of Your Feedback

> **Time: 5 minutes | LO1–LO4**

Receiving feedback on a formal assessment can feel daunting; however, research in educational psychology consistently demonstrates that structured review of errors is one of the most effective strategies for improving performance (Hattie and Timperley, 2007). This session treats the mid-term assessment not as a verdict, but as diagnostic data — a map showing exactly where effort should be concentrated.

The mid-term assessment covered four learning outcomes (LO1–LO4), which together span approximately 20 weeks of taught content. The diagram below summarises what each learning outcome required.

```
+----------------------------------------------------------+
|              MID-TERM ASSESSMENT COVERAGE                |
+----------+---------------------------------------------+
|   LO1    |  Project Stages: Initiation → Planning →   |
|          |  Implementation → Closure                  |
+----------+---------------------------------------------+
|   LO2    |  Roles (sponsor, PM, board) and Skills     |
|          |  (planning, leadership, team building…)    |
+----------+---------------------------------------------+
|   LO3    |  Tools: Gantt Charts, CPA, PERT            |
+----------+---------------------------------------------+
|   LO4    |  Information: Types, Sources, Research,    |
|          |  Validity, Collation                       |
+----------+---------------------------------------------+
```

The assessment data gathered across multiple examination series (OCR, 2022–2024) shows that students most frequently drop marks in three areas:

- **Lack of contextualisation** — giving a generic answer rather than one linked to the scenario.
- **Incomplete answers** — failing to explain *why* something matters, not just *what* it is.
- **Calculation errors** — particularly in CPA forward/backward passes and PERT formula application.

Each of these pitfalls is addressed directly in the sections that follow.

> **Bloom's Taxonomy reminder:** OCR exam questions progress from *knowledge* (1–2 marks), through *application* (3–6 marks), to *evaluation* (6–12 marks). Marks are lost most heavily at the evaluation level because students revert to description rather than justified analysis (OCR, 2023).

## Section 1: LO1 Review — Project Stages & Planning

> **Time: 12 minutes | LO1.1, LO1.2, LO1.3, LO1.4**

### 1.1 Overview of LO1

LO1 requires students to understand all four sequential stages of engineering project management. The unit specification identifies these as: **initiation**, **planning**, **implementation**, and **closure** (OCR, 2017). The weighting for LO1 in the external examination is 10–30% of available marks (OCR, 2017), meaning it carries significant assessment weight.

```
PROJECT LIFECYCLE — LO1 OVERVIEW
==================================
  STAGE 1      STAGE 2       STAGE 3        STAGE 4
 INITIATION --> PLANNING --> IMPLEMENTATION --> CLOSURE
     |              |              |               |
  Proposal       SMART          Issue logs     Final report
  Feasibility    Resources       Monitoring     Lessons learned
  Controls       Stakeholders    Corrective     Sign-off
  Comms plan     Risk            action
```

### 1.2 Common Errors in LO1

The following errors appear frequently across examination cohorts (OCR mark schemes, 2022–2024):

**Error 1 — Confusing initiation with planning**

Students often describe planning activities (e.g., Gantt charts, resource plans) when asked about the *initiation* stage. The initiation stage is concerned with establishing whether the project is viable and defining its high-level parameters, not with detailed scheduling.

> **Correct understanding:** The project proposal, feasibility study, communication strategy, and project controls (time, cost, quality) all belong to the *initiation* stage (OCR, 2017).

**Error 2 — SMART objectives given without explanation**

When asked to describe an outline plan objective, students frequently write "the objective must be SMART" without elaborating. Examiners require students to explain what each SMART criterion means and how it applies to the specific scenario.

```
SMART — WHAT EXAMINERS EXPECT
=================================
S = Specific      → clearly defined, not vague
M = Measurable    → quantified so progress can be tracked
A = Achievable    → realistic given the available resources
R = Relevant      → aligned to the project's stated purpose
T = Time-bound    → assigned a clear deadline
```

> **Example (engineering context):** A SMART objective for a new CNC machine installation project might be: *"Install and commission two CNC lathes in the Sheffield manufacturing facility to a production rate of 120 units per hour by 31 July 2026, within a budget of £85,000."* (PMI, 2021)

**Error 3 — Lessons learned placed in the wrong stage**

Lessons learned are a closure activity (LO1.4), not a planning activity. Students who associate lessons learned with Week 2 planning content regularly misplace this in examination answers.

**Error 4 — Generic answers lacking contextualisation**

OCR mark schemes consistently award additional marks for answers that explicitly reference the scenario. For example, when asked why quality control is important *for this specific project*, a generic answer ("quality is important so the product is good") earns fewer marks than a contextualised answer that references the project type, the client's requirements, or the engineering standards applicable (OCR, 2022).

### 1.3 Remediation Exercise — LO1

Study the scenario below and answer the question that follows.

> *Meridian Rail Engineering Ltd is planning to upgrade the signalling system on a 14-kilometre stretch of track in the East Midlands. The project has a fixed budget of £2.4 million and must be completed within 18 months to comply with a Network Rail regulatory deadline.*

**Question:** Identify and describe **two** topics that should be included in the project's outline plan. *(4 marks)*

> 📌 **Lecturer:** Allow 3 minutes for students to attempt this individually, then take two or three responses from the group. Highlight contextualised answers on the board. Model answer: *Scope* — defines what work is included/excluded (relevant here because the project boundary, i.e., 14 km of track, must be clearly defined to prevent scope creep given the fixed budget). *Constraints* — documents limitations such as the £2.4m budget and 18-month deadline imposed by Network Rail.

## Section 2: LO2 Review — Roles & Skills

> **Time: 10 minutes | LO2.1, LO2.2**

### 2.1 Overview of LO2

LO2 covers two distinct areas: the formal **roles** within a project organisation (LO2.1), and the **personal skills** required of an effective project manager (LO2.2). The unit specification identifies ten key PM skills: planning, time management, decision-making, communication, negotiation, delegation, leadership, team building, conflict management, and information management (OCR, 2017).

```
LO2 STRUCTURE
==============================
LO2.1 — ROLES            LO2.2 — SKILLS
--------------            --------------
Project Sponsor           Planning
Project Board             Time Management
Project Manager           Decision-Making
Team Leaders              Communication
Team Members              Negotiation
End Users                 Delegation
External Stakeholders     Leadership
                          Team Building
                          Conflict Management
                          Information Management
```

### 2.2 Common Errors in LO2

**Error 1 — Muddling the project sponsor with the project manager**

Examination answers frequently assign the project sponsor's responsibilities to the project manager. The distinction is critical: the **project sponsor** authorises the project, provides funding, and champions the project at board level, whereas the **project manager** is responsible for day-to-day delivery (APM, 2019). In examination scenarios, the sponsor's name typically appears as the most senior figure and the project manager's name as the person running meetings and producing reports.

**Error 2 — Describing leadership styles without applying them**

Questions on leadership styles (directive, supportive, collaborative, responsive) routinely ask students to *analyse* the impact of a chosen style on the project. Students who simply define the style without explaining its consequences on team performance, project risk, or stakeholder relationships earn only partial credit.

```
LEADERSHIP STYLES — KEY FEATURES
=========================================
DIRECTIVE    | High control, clear instructions
             | Suited to new/inexperienced teams
-----------  +---------------------------------
SUPPORTIVE   | Relationship-focused, empathetic
             | Suited to motivated but anxious teams
-----------  +---------------------------------
COLLABORATIVE| Shared decision-making, consensus
             | Suited to expert teams on complex tasks
-----------  +---------------------------------
RESPONSIVE   | Hands-off, delegates fully
             | Suited to highly experienced, self-managing teams
```

> **Source:** Hersey and Blanchard (1969), as applied in project management contexts by Turner (2014).

**Error 3 — Confusing features of skills with their benefits**

When asked to explain how a skill *benefits* a project, students often describe what the skill *involves* instead. For example, describing "delegation" as "giving tasks to team members" is a feature; explaining that delegation "allows the project manager to focus on high-priority risks, thereby reducing the likelihood of schedule overrun" is a benefit (OCR mark schemes, 2022–2024).

### 2.3 Remediation Exercise — LO2

> *Lena is a project manager overseeing the installation of a new automated welding line at a Birmingham automotive components factory. Her team includes four highly experienced senior engineers who have managed similar installations before. However, Lena insists on approving every decision herself and holds three-hour status meetings daily.*

**Question:** Identify the leadership style Lena is currently using and analyse **one** impact this approach is likely to have on the project outcome. *(3 marks)*

> 📌 **Model answer:** Directive leadership (1 mark). Because the team members are highly experienced, a directive approach is mismatched to their capability level (Hersey and Blanchard, 1969), which is likely to cause frustration and disengagement (1 mark), ultimately reducing team productivity and increasing the risk of key personnel leaving the project (1 mark). This could lead to delays and cost overruns due to loss of specialist expertise.

## Section 3: LO3 Review — Project Management Tools

> **Time: 13 minutes | LO3.1, LO3.2, LO3.3**

### 3.1 Overview of LO3

LO3 is the most heavily calculation-based learning outcome in the unit. Students are assessed on three tools: **Gantt charts** (LO3.1), **Critical Path Analysis (CPA)** (LO3.2), and the **Programme Evaluation and Review Technique (PERT)** (LO3.3). Collectively, these tools can account for up to 23% of examination marks (OCR, 2017). Accuracy in applying formulae and correctly constructing network diagrams is essential.

### 3.2 Common Errors in LO3

#### 3.2a Gantt Charts (LO3.1)

**Error 1 — Misidentifying concurrent activities**
Students sometimes treat all activities as sequential. Examiners expect students to recognise that activities with no dependency relationship can run in parallel, which reduces overall project duration.

**Error 2 — Confusing features with benefits**
A common pitfall is describing what a Gantt chart *shows* rather than how it *helps*. The examiner expects a statement of benefit — for example, a Gantt chart showing activity dependencies *helps* the project manager identify which delays will affect the completion date, enabling proactive rescheduling (OCR, 2023).

---

#### 3.2b Critical Path Analysis (LO3.2)

CPA is consistently one of the highest-mark topics in the examination (OCR, 2022–2024). Errors fall into three categories.

**Error 1 — Incorrect node notation**

```
CORRECT NODE FORMAT (Activity on Arrow)
==========================================
        EST              LFT
        [  ]            [  ]
   _____|_____ Activity  _____|_____
  |    Node   |-------->|    Node   |
  |___________|          |___________|
      Duration
```

Students frequently place values in the wrong position within the node, or confuse **Earliest Start Time (EST)** with **Latest Finish Time (LFT)**.

**Error 2 — Errors in the forward and backward pass**

Forward pass: work left to right, always take the **maximum** value when paths merge.  
Backward pass: work right to left, always take the **minimum** value when paths diverge.

```
FORWARD / BACKWARD PASS RULES
================================
Direction      | Rule at a MERGE node
---------------+----------------------
FORWARD pass   | Take the MAXIMUM
BACKWARD pass  | Take the MINIMUM
```

> **Example:** If two paths arrive at Node 5, giving ESTs of 14 and 17, the EST at Node 5 is **17** (the maximum).

**Error 3 — Incorrect float calculation**

Float = LFT − EST − Duration

Students who subtract only LFT − EST (forgetting to deduct activity duration) regularly calculate incorrect float values, leading to a misidentified critical path.

```
FLOAT CALCULATION
==================
Float = LFT - EST - Duration

Critical path activities have Float = 0
```

---

#### 3.2c PERT (LO3.3)

The PERT formula is directly assessed in most examination series (OCR, 2022–2024). Students are expected to recall and apply it accurately.

```
PERT FORMULA
=============

       O + 4M + P
  E = -----------
            6

Where:
  O = Optimistic time estimate
  M = Most likely time estimate
  P = Pessimistic time estimate
  E = Expected (weighted average) duration
```

> **Source:** PMI (2021). *A Guide to the Project Management Body of Knowledge (PMBOK® Guide)*, 7th edition. Project Management Institute.

**Error 1 — Using simple average instead of weighted average**
Students who calculate (O + M + P) ÷ 3 instead of (O + 4M + P) ÷ 6 obtain an incorrect result and lose all calculation marks. The factor of 4 applied to M reflects the fact that the most likely estimate is the best-informed one and should carry the greatest weight (PMI, 2021).

**Error 2 — Incorrect derivation of O and P from percentage-based problems**
Examination questions often provide the most likely time and then state that O is a percentage *below* M and P is a percentage *above* M. Students must calculate O and P before substituting into the formula.

### 3.3 Worked Example — PERT

> *An engineering team estimates that installing a new compressed air system will most likely take 10 weeks. The optimistic estimate is 70% of the most likely time, and the pessimistic estimate is 160% of the most likely time. Calculate the expected duration using PERT.*

**Step 1 — Derive O and P:**
```
O = 70% of 10  = 0.70 × 10 = 7 weeks
P = 160% of 10 = 1.60 × 10 = 16 weeks
M = 10 weeks
```

**Step 2 — Apply the formula:**
```
     O + 4M + P     7 + (4 × 10) + 16     7 + 40 + 16     63
E = ------------ = ------------------- = ------------- = ---- = 10.5 weeks
          6                 6                    6           6
```

**Answer:** The expected project duration is **10.5 weeks**.

> 📌 **Lecturer:** Invite a student to attempt a similar calculation on the board before moving on. Use the June 2024 examination paper Question 3(c) as the source question.

## Section 4: LO4 Review — Information Management

> **Time: 10 minutes | LO4.1, LO4.2, LO4.3, LO4.4, LO4.5**

### 4.1 Overview of LO4

LO4 covers the entire information management cycle: understanding **types** of information (LO4.1), identifying appropriate **sources** (LO4.2), selecting **research methods** (LO4.3), judging **validity** (LO4.4), and **collating and presenting** findings to support decisions (LO4.5). The unit specification places particular emphasis on students being able to evaluate the reliability and usefulness of information in the context of a specific engineering project (OCR, 2017).

```
LO4 — INFORMATION MANAGEMENT CYCLE
======================================
  LO4.1        LO4.2        LO4.3
  TYPES   -->  SOURCES  --> RESEARCH
  (what)       (where)      (how)
                               |
                               v
                           LO4.4
                          VALIDITY
                          (is it trustworthy?)
                               |
                               v
                           LO4.5
                          COLLATION
                          (present & decide)
```

### 4.2 Common Errors in LO4

**Error 1 — Mixing up qualitative and quantitative information**

Students frequently confuse these two types when applied to engineering scenarios.

```
QUALITATIVE vs QUANTITATIVE
==============================
QUALITATIVE          | QUANTITATIVE
---------------------|-------------------
Descriptive/opinion  | Numerical/measurable
e.g. "stakeholders   | e.g. "cost overrun
feel the project     | was £45,000 (12%
is behind schedule"  | above budget)"
```

> **Key rule:** If it can be counted or measured, it is quantitative. If it is based on judgement, perception, or description, it is qualitative (OCR, 2017).

**Error 2 — Describing research methods without explaining their limitations**

Examination questions on LO4.3 almost always require students to evaluate a research method — i.e., to explain both its benefit *and* its limitation in the context of the given project. Students who only list the benefit lose half the available marks.

```
PRIMARY vs SECONDARY RESEARCH — SUMMARY
===========================================
             BENEFIT                LIMITATION
PRIMARY      First-hand, specific   Time-consuming,
(e.g. survey  to the project        expensive to conduct
focus group)
-----------  ---------------------  -------------------
SECONDARY    Quick to access,       May be out of date,
(e.g. reports low cost              not specific to the
published data)                     project context
```

> **Source:** Saunders, Lewis and Thornhill (2019). *Research Methods for Business Students*, 8th edition. Pearson.

**Error 3 — Treating all ten validity criteria as interchangeable**

LO4.4 lists ten criteria for judging information validity: integrity of source, bias, relevance, complexity, degree of detail, currency, quality, accuracy, reliability, and importance (OCR, 2017). Students who define "reliability" as "the information is accurate" confuse two distinct criteria. In examination answers:

- **Reliability** means the research can be *reproduced* — repeating the study yields the same results (Saunders, Lewis and Thornhill, 2019).
- **Accuracy** means the information is *correct* and free from errors.
- **Currency** means the information is *up to date* and still applicable.

**Error 4 — Weak collation answers (LO4.5)**

Questions on LO4.5 ask students to organise and present data to support a decision. Common weaknesses include: presenting raw data without interpretation, failing to draw a conclusion, or not linking the data back to the project's objectives. Hague (2016) stresses that data only adds value when it drives a justified decision — "presenting for its own sake" does not satisfy the examiner's requirement.

### 4.3 Remediation Exercise — LO4

Study the following data table and answer the question below.

```
QUALITY INSPECTION DATA — Meridian Rail Signalling Project
(Weeks 1–4 of implementation)

Week | Inspections | Non-conformances | Defect Rate (%)
-----+-------------+------------------+----------------
  1  |     50      |        4         |      8.0
  2  |     50      |        6         |     12.0
  3  |     50      |        3         |      6.0
  4  |     50      |        5         |     10.0
-----+-------------+------------------+----------------
     Project quality target: ≤ 5.0% defect rate
```

**Question:** Using the data above, evaluate whether the project is meeting its quality target. Justify your answer with reference to the data. *(3 marks)*

> 📌 **Model answer:** The project is **not** meeting its quality target (1 mark). The mean defect rate over four weeks is (8.0 + 12.0 + 6.0 + 10.0) ÷ 4 = **9.0%**, which exceeds the target of 5.0% by 4.0 percentage points (1 mark). Furthermore, all four individual weeks exceed the target, indicating a consistent systemic problem rather than an isolated anomaly, and the rate shows no downward trend, suggesting corrective action has not yet been effective (1 mark).

## Section 5: Personalised Improvement Plans

> **Time: 5 minutes | LO1–LO4**

### 5.1 Why Improvement Plans Matter

Research by Black and Wiliam (1998) demonstrates that feedback is only effective when the learner acts upon it. Simply reviewing where marks were lost is insufficient; students must commit to specific, targeted revision actions. The improvement plan template below structures this process.

### 5.2 How to Complete the Plan

Each student should complete the template using their returned script as reference. The plan should be specific enough that another person could verify its completion — vague statements such as "revise CPA" are insufficient; a specific action such as "complete three CPA forward/backward pass exercises from past papers, checking against mark schemes" is actionable.

## Conclusion

> **Time: 2 minutes | LO1–LO4**

This session has systematically revisited the four learning outcomes assessed in the mid-term examination. The key messages are:

1. **Contextualise every answer.** Generic responses earn fewer marks. Every answer should reference the specific project scenario provided.
2. **Explain consequences, not just definitions.** At application and evaluation level, the examiner rewards answers that link cause to effect.
3. **Accuracy in calculations is non-negotiable.** For LO3, one arithmetic error can cascade through subsequent calculation steps. Check each value before proceeding.
4. **Use the ten validity criteria precisely.** Reliability, accuracy, and currency are distinct — do not treat them as synonyms.
5. **Act on feedback.** The personalised improvement plan completed today represents a commitment to targeted revision that will directly raise performance in the external examination.

> *"The secret of getting ahead is getting started."* — attributed to Mark Twain

Students are encouraged to visit their assigned learning support resources and to make use of past examination papers alongside published OCR mark schemes to practise under timed conditions.
