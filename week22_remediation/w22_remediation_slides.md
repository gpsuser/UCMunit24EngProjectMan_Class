# Introduction: Making Sense of Your Feedback

> *Structured review of errors is one of the most effective strategies for improving performance* — Hattie & Timperley (2007)

**Your mid-term result is diagnostic data** — a map showing where to focus, not a verdict.

Three patterns appear across all exam cohorts (OCR, 2022–2024):

| Common Pitfall | What it looks like in answers |
|----------------|-------------------------------|
| **Lack of contextualisation** | Generic answer with no link to the scenario |
| **Incomplete answers** | Defines *what* something is; never explains *why* it matters |
| **Calculation errors** | Particularly CPA forward/backward pass and PERT formula |

# Section 1: LO1 Review
## Project Stages & Planning
### 12 minutes

---

# LO1 — The Project Lifecycle

```
PROJECT LIFECYCLE — LO1 OVERVIEW
==================================
  STAGE 1      STAGE 2       STAGE 3        STAGE 4
 INITIATION --> PLANNING --> IMPLEMENTATION --> CLOSURE
     |              |              |               |
  Proposal       SMART          Issue logs     Final report
  Feasibility    Resources      Monitoring     Lessons learned
  Controls       Stakeholders   Corrective     Sign-off
  Comms plan     Risk           action
```

LO1 carries **10-30% of available marks** (OCR, 2017).
Accuracy about which activities belong to which stage is essential.

---

# LO1 — Error 1: Initiation vs Planning

**Most common mistake:** describing planning activities (e.g. Gantt charts) when asked about the *initiation* stage.

### What belongs to Initiation:

- **Project proposal** — makes the case for the project
- **Feasibility study** — assesses viability
- **Communication strategy** — who needs what information, when
- **Project controls** — defining time, cost and quality parameters

> **Rule of thumb:**
> Initiation answers *"Should we do this project?"*
> Planning answers *"How will we do it?"*

---

# LO1 — Error 2: SMART Objectives

**The mistake:** writing "the objective must be SMART" without explaining each criterion in context.

```
SMART — WHAT EXAMINERS EXPECT
=================================
S = Specific      --> clearly defined, not vague
M = Measurable    --> quantified so progress can be tracked
A = Achievable    --> realistic given the available resources
R = Relevant      --> aligned to the project's stated purpose
T = Time-bound    --> assigned a clear deadline
```

> **Example (engineering context):**
> "Install and commission two CNC lathes in the Sheffield facility
> to 120 units/hour by 31 July 2026, within a budget of £85,000."

Each criterion must be **applied to the scenario** — not just listed.

---

# LO1 — Errors 3 & 4

### Error 3 — Lessons learned in the wrong stage

**Lessons learned belong to CLOSURE (Stage 4)** — not planning.
A common misplacement in examination answers.

### Error 4 — Generic answers

| Generic *(fewer marks)* | Contextualised *(full marks)* |
|-------------------------|-------------------------------|
| "Quality is important so the product is good" | References the specific client requirements, project type, or applicable engineering standards from the scenario |

> **Every answer should reference the scenario.**
> Link your answer to the named project, client, or constraint in the question.

```
LO2 STRUCTURE
==============================

--------------            --------------
Project Sponsor           Planning
Project Board             Time Management
Project Manager           Decision-Making
Team Leaders              Communication

External Stakeholders     Leadership
                          Team Building
                          Conflict Management

```

> The unit specification identifies **ten** key PM skills (OCR, 2017). Know each one.

---

# LO2 — Error 1: Sponsor vs Project Manager

**Mistake:** assigning the sponsor's responsibilities to the project manager.

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|---------------|
| **Project Sponsor** | Authorises the project, provides funding, champions it at board level |
| **Project Manager** | Responsible for day-to-day delivery, running meetings, producing reports |

> In exam scenarios:
> **Sponsor** = most senior named figure
> **Project manager** = person running day-to-day operations (APM, 2019)

---

# LO2 — Error 2: Leadership Styles

**Mistake:** defining a style without explaining its impact on the project.

```
LEADERSHIP STYLES — KEY FEATURES
=========================================
DIRECTIVE    | High control, clear instructions
             | Suited to new / inexperienced teams
-----------  +---------------------------------
SUPPORTIVE   | Relationship-focused, empathetic
             | Suited to motivated but anxious teams
-----------  +---------------------------------
COLLABORATIVE| Shared decision-making, consensus
             | Suited to expert teams on complex tasks
-----------  +---------------------------------
RESPONSIVE   | Hands-off, delegates fully
             | Suited to highly experienced, self-managing teams
```

> Questions ask you to *analyse the impact* — reference team experience level
> and explain consequences for project performance (Hersey & Blanchard, 1969).

---

# LO2 — Error 3: Features vs Benefits of Skills

**Mistake:** describing what a skill *involves* rather than how it *helps* the project.

| Skill | Feature *(insufficient)* | Benefit *(full credit)* |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------------|
| **Delegation** | "Giving tasks to team members" | "Allows the PM to focus on high-priority risks, reducing the likelihood of schedule overrun" |
| **Communication** | "Sharing information with the team" | "Ensures stakeholders are aligned, preventing costly rework from misunderstood requirements" |

> **The examiner rewards cause to effect reasoning.**
> Ask: *"So what? Why does this matter for the project?"* (OCR, 2022–2024)

# Section 3: LO3 Review
## Project Management Tools
### 13 minutes

---

# LO3 — Tools Overview & Gantt Chart Errors

LO3 can account for up to **23% of examination marks** (OCR, 2017).

| Tool | LO | Typical exam focus |
|------|----|--------------------|
| **Gantt Chart** | LO3.1 | Concurrent activities; features vs benefits |
| **Critical Path Analysis** | LO3.2 | Forward/backward pass; float; critical path |
| **PERT** | LO3.3 | Weighted average formula; deriving O and P |

### Gantt Chart errors

- **Error 1:** treating all activities as sequential — activities without a dependency can run **in parallel**, reducing project duration
- **Error 2:** describing what a Gantt chart *shows* (feature) rather than how it *helps* the project manager (benefit)

---

# LO3 — CPA: Node Notation & Pass Rules

```
CORRECT NODE FORMAT (Activity on Arrow)
=========================================
       EST               LFT
      [   ]             [   ]
 ______|____             ______|____
|  Node A   |--Dur.---->|  Node B   |
|___________|           |___________|
```

**Error 1:** values in the wrong position — EST goes **left**, LFT goes **right**.

```
FORWARD / BACKWARD PASS RULES
================================
Direction        | Rule at a MERGE node
-----------------+------------------------
FORWARD pass     | Take the MAXIMUM value
BACKWARD pass    | Take the MINIMUM value
```

> **Example:** Two paths give ESTs of 14 and 17 at Node 5.
> EST at Node 5 = **17** (always take the maximum on the forward pass).

---

# LO3 — CPA: Float & the Critical Path

```
FLOAT CALCULATION
==================
Float = LFT - EST - Duration

Critical path activities --> Float = 0
```

**Error 3 — Forgetting to subtract Duration**

Students who calculate LFT minus EST only (omitting Duration) produce incorrect float values and misidentify the critical path.

> **Check your work:**
> Every activity on the critical path must have **Float = 0**.
> If Float is not equal to 0 on a critical path activity, an arithmetic error has been made.

---

# LO3 — The PERT Formula

```
PERT WEIGHTED AVERAGE FORMULA
================================

       O + 4M + P
  E = -----------
            6

  O = Optimistic time estimate
  M = Most likely time estimate
  P = Pessimistic time estimate
  E = Expected (weighted average) duration
```

**Why the factor of 4?** The most likely estimate is the best-informed one and carries the greatest weight (PMI, 2021).

> **Error 1 — Using a simple average:**
> Calculating (O + M + P) divided by 3 is incorrect — all calculation marks are lost.

---

# LO3 — PERT: Worked Example

> *Most likely duration: 10 weeks. Optimistic = 70% of M. Pessimistic = 160% of M.*

**Step 1 — Derive O and P first:**

```
O = 70%  of 10 = 0.70 x 10 =  7 weeks
P = 160% of 10 = 1.60 x 10 = 16 weeks
M =                           10 weeks
```

**Step 2 — Apply the formula:**

```
     O + 4M + P     7 + (4 x 10) + 16     7 + 40 + 16     63
E = ------------ = ------------------- = ------------- = ---- = 10.5 weeks
          6                 6                    6           6
```

**Answer: Expected duration = 10.5 weeks**

> **Error 2:** Forgetting to derive O and P before substituting is one of the most avoidable mistakes in LO3.

# LO4 — Qualitative vs Quantitative

```
QUALITATIVE vs QUANTITATIVE
==============================
QUALITATIVE           | QUANTITATIVE
----------------------+---------------------
Descriptive/opinion   | Numerical/measurable
"Stakeholders feel    | "Cost overrun was
the project is        | £45,000 (12% above
behind schedule"      | budget)"
```

> **Key rule:** If it can be **counted or measured** — it is quantitative.
> If based on **judgement, perception, or description** — it is qualitative (OCR, 2017).

---

# LO4 — Research Methods: Benefits & Limitations

```
PRIMARY vs SECONDARY RESEARCH
===========================================
              BENEFIT               LIMITATION
PRIMARY       First-hand data,      Time-consuming
(survey,      specific to the       and expensive
focus group)  project               to conduct
-----------   --------------------  ----------------
SECONDARY     Quick to access,      May be out of date;
(published    low cost              not specific to the
reports)                            project context
```

> Exam questions **almost always ask you to evaluate** — explain both the **benefit** AND the **limitation** (Saunders, Lewis & Thornhill, 2019).
> Listing only a benefit loses approximately half the available marks.

---

# LO4 — Validity Criteria & Collation

### Three most frequently confused criteria (LO4.4)

| Criterion | Precise meaning |
|-----------|----------------|
| **Reliability** | Research can be *reproduced* — repeating the study gives the same result |
| **Accuracy** | The information is *correct* and free from errors |
| **Currency** | The information is *up to date* and still applicable |

LO4.4 lists **ten** validity criteria — each is distinct; never conflate them (OCR, 2017).

### LO4.5 — A strong collation answer requires three elements:

1. **Observation** — what does the data show?
2. **Calculation** — what does the maths confirm?
3. **Justified decision** — what action does this support, and why?

# Conclusion

### Five messages to take away:

1. **Contextualise every answer** — generic responses earn fewer marks; always reference the scenario.
2. **Explain consequences, not just definitions** — link cause to effect at application and evaluation level.
3. **Accuracy in calculations is non-negotiable** — one CPA or PERT error cascades through every step.
4. **Use validity criteria precisely** — reliability, accuracy and currency are distinct; never conflate them.
5. **Act on feedback** — complete your improvement plan and bring it to Week 23.

> *"The secret of getting ahead is getting started."*

Use past examination papers and OCR mark schemes to practise under timed conditions.
