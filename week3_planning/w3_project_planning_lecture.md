## 2. Introduction: Why Planning Matters

*(5 minutes | LO1.2)*

Planning is the second stage of the project lifecycle, following initiation. Whereas initiation establishes *what* the project is and *why* it is needed, planning establishes *how* the project will be delivered (Project Management Institute, 2021).

The consequences of poor planning are well documented in UK engineering. The original Wembley Stadium rebuild (completed 2007) is a frequently cited example: the project overran its original £757 million budget by more than £400 million, in part due to inadequate resource and financial planning at the outset (Flyvbjerg, Bruzelius and Rothengatter, 2003). Similarly, the early phases of the HS2 programme revealed cost estimates that escalated sharply once detailed resource plans were produced, illustrating the direct financial consequences of under-planning.

> **Key principle:** Time invested in thorough planning reduces the likelihood of cost overruns, schedule delays and quality failures during project implementation (Turner, 2014).

The planning stage produces a suite of interrelated documents. This session focuses on three of the most fundamental:

```
+---------------------+
|    PLANNING STAGE   |
+---------------------+
         |
    _____|______
   |     |      |
   v     v      v
Outline Resource Financial
 Plan    Plan    Plan
```

*Note: Risk analysis, quality planning, stakeholder analysis, and project scheduling are addressed separately in Week 4.*

## 3. The Outline Plan

*(12 minutes | LO1.2)*

### 3.1 What Is an Outline Plan?

The outline plan is the foundational planning document. It provides a concise, structured summary of the project and sets the framework within which all other planning activities take place (Association for Project Management, 2019). It is sometimes referred to as a project brief or project definition document.

### 3.2 Components of the Outline Plan

The OCR Unit 24 specification identifies nine components that learners must be able to describe (OCR, 2017):

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Purpose** | The reason the project exists — the problem being solved or opportunity being pursued |
| **Aims** | The broad, high-level goals of the project |
| **Objectives** | Specific, measurable targets that define project success (must be SMART — see Section 4) |
| **Scope** | The defined boundaries of the project: what is included and, importantly, what is excluded |
| **Constraints** | Limitations on the project, such as fixed budget, non-negotiable deadlines, or regulatory requirements |
| **Deliverables** | The tangible outputs the project must produce (e.g., a commissioned machine, an installed system, a qualified workforce) |
| **Targets/Goals** | Specific performance benchmarks the project must achieve |
| **Communication** | The strategy for how information will flow to and between stakeholders |
| **Controls** | The mechanisms used to keep the project on track (time, cost and quality) |

> **Exam tip:** Questions may ask learners to *describe* a component — this requires explaining what it means, not just naming it. For example, stating "scope defines the boundaries of the project, including what is excluded" is worth more than simply writing "scope."

### 3.3 Aims vs Objectives: An Important Distinction

A common source of confusion in examination answers is conflating *aims* and *objectives*. The distinction is as follows:

- **Aims** are broad, directional statements of intent. They describe the overall aspiration of the project.
- **Objectives** are specific, measurable and time-bound statements derived from the aims.

**Example (Crossrail / Elizabeth Line):**

| | Statement |
|-|-----------|
| **Aim** | Increase rail capacity across Greater London |
| **Objective** | Achieve a passenger capacity of 200 million journeys per year by December 2022 |

## 4. SMART Objectives

*(10 minutes | LO1.2)*

### 4.1 The SMART Framework

Objectives that are vague or unmeasurable cannot be used to assess whether a project has succeeded. The SMART framework provides a structured method for writing robust project objectives (Doran, 1981).

```
S  --  Specific
M  --  Measurable
A  --  Agreed
R  --  Realistic
T  --  Time-specific
```

The OCR Unit 24 specification uses this precise formulation. Note that the 'A' stands for **Agreed** (not 'Achievable', as used in some other contexts) — this emphasises that objectives must be accepted and committed to by the relevant stakeholders (OCR, 2017).

### 4.2 SMART Criteria Explained

| Letter | Criterion | Meaning | Weak Example | Strong (SMART) Example |
|--------|-----------|---------|--------------|------------------------|
| **S** | Specific | The objective is clearly defined with no ambiguity | "Improve the production line" | "Reduce assembly cycle time on Line 3 at the Sunderland plant" |
| **M** | Measurable | Success can be quantified and verified | "Cut costs" | "Reduce assembly cycle time by 15%" |
| **A** | Agreed | All key stakeholders have accepted the objective | Imposed by management without consultation | Formally signed off by engineering manager and project sponsor |
| **R** | Realistic | Achievable given available resources and constraints | "Reduce cycle time by 90% in one month" | "Reduce cycle time by 15% within six months" |
| **T** | Time-specific | A clear deadline or timeframe is stated | "As soon as possible" | "By 30 June 2026" |

### 4.3 Full SMART Objective Example

**Engineering context:** A UK automotive manufacturer is introducing a new robotic welding system.

> *"Reduce the defect rate on the body-in-white welding assembly at the Birmingham plant by 20%, as agreed by the production director and the project sponsor, without additional capital expenditure, by 31 March 2027."*

This objective satisfies all five SMART criteria:
- **Specific:** welding assembly, Birmingham plant
- **Measurable:** 20% reduction in defect rate
- **Agreed:** production director and project sponsor
- **Realistic:** no additional capital expenditure required
- **Time-specific:** 31 March 2027

## 5. The Resource Plan

*(12 minutes | LO1.2)*

### 5.1 Purpose of the Resource Plan

The resource plan identifies what resources are required, where they will be sourced, and when they are needed (OCR, 2017). Without a resource plan, a project risks delays caused by unavailable equipment, missing materials, or insufficient staffing.

The resource plan answers three essential questions:

```
+-----------+     +----------+     +----------+
|   WHAT?   |     |  WHERE?  |     |   WHEN?  |
| Resources |     | Location |     | Timings  |
| required  |     | & source |     | required |
+-----------+     +----------+     +----------+
```

### 5.2 The Three Categories of Resource

The Unit 24 specification identifies three categories of resource that must be considered (OCR, 2017):

#### (a) Physical Resources

Physical resources are the tangible assets and materials required to execute the project.

| Sub-category | Examples |
|--------------|----------|
| Equipment | CNC machines, welding rigs, testing apparatus, cranes |
| Materials | Steel, composites, electronic components, consumables |
| Facilities | Workshop space, storage, site compound |
| Tools | Hand tools, calibrated instruments, safety equipment |

> **Planning consideration:** Physical resources must be scheduled precisely. For a bridge construction project, steel sections must arrive on site at the exact point they are needed — too early creates storage problems and weather-damage risk; too late stalls the programme.

#### (b) Technological Resources

Technological resources encompass the digital tools, software systems and data infrastructure required by the project.

| Sub-category | Examples |
|--------------|----------|
| Software | CAD/CAM packages, project management software (e.g., MS Project), simulation tools |
| IT infrastructure | Servers, networks, cloud storage |
| Specialist systems | SCADA systems, embedded control systems, testing rigs |
| Data resources | Technical databases, standards libraries (e.g., BS EN standards) |

#### (c) Human Resources

Human resources are the people whose skills and time are required by the project.

| Sub-category | Examples |
|--------------|----------|
| Core team | Project manager, design engineers, technicians |
| Specialists | Structural engineers, electrical engineers, NDT inspectors |
| External contractors | Subcontractors, consultants |
| Support staff | Administrative staff, procurement officers |

> **Exam tip:** Examination questions often require learners to distinguish between the three resource categories with specific examples. Generic answers such as "people and equipment" are unlikely to gain full marks. Answers must name specific resource types in context.

### 5.3 Resource Plan Example: Rolls-Royce Engine Test Facility Upgrade

To illustrate how a resource plan is structured, consider a simplified extract for an upgrade to a test cell facility:

| Resource Category | Resource | Where Sourced | When Required |
|-------------------|----------|---------------|---------------|
| Physical | High-pressure fuel manifold assemblies | Tier 1 supplier, Derby | Week 4 of project |
| Physical | Acoustic lining panels | In-house fabrication, Hucknall | Weeks 6–8 |
| Technological | Thermal imaging diagnostic software | Vendor licence | Week 1 (before testing commences) |
| Human | NDT inspection engineers (x2) | External contractor | Weeks 10–12 |
| Human | Commissioning technician | Internal redeployment | Weeks 9–14 |

## 6. The Financial Plan

*(12 minutes | LO1.2)*

### 6.1 Purpose of the Financial Plan

The financial plan defines how the project will be funded and how expenditure will be managed over time. It provides the financial baseline against which actual spend is later monitored (Turner, 2014). Without a robust financial plan, projects are susceptible to budget overruns and cash flow problems.

The three main components of a financial plan are:

```
+------------------+
|  FINANCIAL PLAN  |
+------------------+
         |
   ______|______
  |      |      |
  v      v      v
Budget  Cost  Sources
Spend   &     of
Plan   Pricing Finance
```

### 6.2 Budget Spend Plan

The budget spend plan maps expenditure across the timeline of the project. It identifies:
- The total approved budget for the project
- The planned expenditure in each time period (e.g., monthly or per phase)
- The cumulative spend profile over time

A spend profile (also known as an S-curve) typically shows low spend in early phases (design and planning), rising steeply during implementation, and tapering off at closure.

**Simplified spend profile — example:**

| Phase | Planned Spend (£k) | Cumulative Spend (£k) |
|-------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Month 1–2: Planning | 50 | 50 |
| Month 3–6: Procurement | 200 | 250 |
| Month 7–12: Implementation | 600 | 850 |
| Month 13–14: Testing | 100 | 950 |
| Month 15: Closure | 50 | 1,000 |

### 6.3 Costing and Pricing

**Unit costs** refer to the cost of a single unit of resource — for example, the cost of one hour of a contractor's time, or the cost per tonne of structural steel.

**Total costs** are derived by multiplying the unit cost by the quantity required:

```
Total Cost = Unit Cost x Quantity Required
```

**Example:**

```
Specialist welding contractor: £85/hour x 320 hours = £27,200
Structural steel sections:     £950/tonne x 45 tonnes = £42,750
```

**Pricing** is relevant where the project delivers outputs for an external client. The project price must cover all costs and typically incorporates a profit margin:

```
Price = Total Cost + Overheads + Profit Margin
```

> **Exam tip:** The specification requires learners to understand the distinction between costing (what it costs to deliver the project) and pricing (what is charged to a client). These are not the same thing.

### 6.4 Sources of Finance

Funding a project requires selecting from a range of financial instruments. The Unit 24 specification requires learners to understand the key features, advantages and disadvantages of different sources (OCR, 2017). These are classified along three dimensions:

**Dimension 1: Internal vs External**

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Internal** | Funding from within the organisation (e.g., retained profits, asset disposal) |
| **External** | Funding from outside the organisation (e.g., bank loans, equity investment) |

**Dimension 2: Short, Medium or Long Term**

| Term | Typical Duration | Example |
|------|-----------------|---------|
| Short-term | Up to 1 year | Overdraft, trade credit |
| Medium-term | 1–5 years | Bank loan, leasing |
| Long-term | 5+ years | Bonds, equity shares |

**Dimension 3: Debt vs Equity Finance**

| Type | Description | Advantage | Disadvantage |
|------|-------------|-----------|--------------|
| **Debt finance** | Borrowed funds that must be repaid with interest (e.g., bank loan) | Organisation retains ownership; interest is tax-deductible | Creates liability; interest must be paid regardless of project outcome |
| **Equity finance** | Funds raised by selling ownership shares in the organisation | No obligation to repay; no interest charges | Dilutes ownership; shareholders expect returns (dividends) |

**Summary of common sources:**

| Source | Type | Term | Advantage | Disadvantage |
|--------|------|------|-----------|--------------|
| Retained profits | Internal | Any | No repayment obligation; no cost | Depletes company reserves |
| Bank loan | External / Debt | Medium–Long | Fixed repayment schedule aids planning | Interest increases total cost; requires security |
| Overdraft | External / Debt | Short | Flexible; quick access | High interest rates; repayable on demand |
| Share issue | External / Equity | Long | No repayment; large sums possible | Dilutes ownership; investor scrutiny |
| Leasing | External / Debt | Medium | Preserves cash; asset maintained by lessor | More expensive than purchase over time; no ownership |
| Government grant | External | Variable | Non-repayable | Competitive; conditions attached |

> **Real-world example:** When Crossrail Ltd required additional funding during the project's extension phase (2018–2022), it drew on a combination of a £1.35 billion loan from the Department for Transport and equity contributions from Transport for London — illustrating the combined use of debt and equity sources in a major engineering project (National Audit Office, 2019).
