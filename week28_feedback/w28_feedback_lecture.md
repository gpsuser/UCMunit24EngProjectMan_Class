## 2. Introduction: Why Measure Success?

> **Time: 5 minutes | LO: LO6.1**

When an engineering project reaches closure, a fundamental question must be answered: *was the project a success?* The answer, however, is rarely as straightforward as it may appear. A project may finish on budget yet still disappoint key stakeholders; conversely, a project may overrun in cost whilst exceeding all quality expectations.

According to the Association for Project Management (APM, 2019), measuring success requires evaluating how well the project's aims and objectives have been met — considering the perspectives of all stakeholders involved, not simply the project manager's view. The APM Body of Knowledge notes that success criteria should be agreed during the planning stage so that meaningful evaluation can take place at closure (APM, 2019).

The Heathrow Terminal 5 project (BAA, 2008) illustrates this well. Delivered on budget and ahead of schedule from a construction perspective, it was widely considered a success by the project team — yet a highly publicised baggage-handling failure at opening led to significant negative media coverage and stakeholder dissatisfaction. This demonstrates that measuring success requires gathering **multiple types of feedback** from **multiple sources** at the point of project completion.

```
PROJECT CLOSURE QUESTION:
"Did the project achieve what it set out to do?"
           |
           v
  +---------------------+
  |   FEEDBACK          |
  |   COLLECTION        |
  +---------------------+
           |
     ______|______
    |             |
    v             v
OBJECTIVE     SUBJECTIVE
(measurable   (opinion,
 facts)        attitudes,
               beliefs)
```

*Figure 1: The two categories of feedback at project closure (OCR, 2017)*

It is important to note that feedback collected for the purpose of **measuring success** is distinct from the ongoing monitoring data gathered during the implementation stage. Completion figures, for example, relate specifically to the **final outcomes** of the entire project, not to the status of individual tasks mid-delivery (OCR, 2017).

## 3. Types of Feedback: Objective vs Subjective

> **Time: 10 minutes | LO: LO6.1**

The OCR Unit Specification (2017) defines two fundamental types of feedback used to measure project success.

### 3.1 Objective Feedback

Objective feedback is **based on measurable facts**. It does not rely on personal opinion; rather, it is derived from data, records, and documented evidence that can be independently verified. Objective feedback provides a factual account of what was achieved against what was planned.

**Examples in engineering projects:**
- Final project cost versus approved budget
- Actual completion date versus planned completion date
- Number of defects recorded against the agreed quality threshold
- Percentage of agreed deliverables accepted by the client

### 3.2 Subjective Feedback

Subjective feedback is **based on opinion, attitudes, beliefs, and reputation**. It reflects how individuals *feel* about the project outcome, the process, or the project team. Whilst subjective feedback does not carry the same evidential weight as measurable data, it is highly valuable because it captures stakeholder experience and perception — dimensions that factual data alone cannot reveal (Kerzner, 2017).

**Examples in engineering projects:**
- A client's informal impression of how well the team communicated
- Media coverage of the Thames Tideway Tunnel project's environmental impact
- A steering committee member's view on the project manager's leadership

### 3.3 A Critical Distinction

It is a common assessment error to confuse the *type* of feedback with the *method* by which it is gathered. Objective feedback may be collected using formal methods (such as a structured review report), whilst subjective feedback may be gathered informally (such as a conversation with an end user). The **type** relates to the *nature of the data*; the **method** relates to *how it is collected*.

```
FEEDBACK TYPES — SUMMARY

+---------------------------+---------------------------+
|      OBJECTIVE            |      SUBJECTIVE           |
+---------------------------+---------------------------+
| Based on measurable facts | Based on opinion/belief   |
| Quantifiable              | Qualitative               |
| Independently verifiable  | Reflects personal view    |
| e.g. cost variance,       | e.g. stakeholder         |
| completion figures,       | satisfaction,             |
| defect counts             | media coverage,           |
|                           | informal chat             |
+---------------------------+---------------------------+
```

*Table 1: Comparison of objective and subjective feedback types (OCR, 2017)*

## 4. Objective Feedback Methods

> **Time: 15 minutes | LO: LO6.2**

The OCR specification (2017) identifies three principal objective feedback methods for engineering projects: **completion figures**, **issue logs**, and **complaints analysis**. Each is examined below.

---

### 4.1 Completion Figures

Completion figures are **quantitative data collected at the end of the entire project** that measure final outcomes against planned targets. They represent the definitive, factual record of project performance in the four key control areas.

**The four core completion figure categories:**

```
COMPLETION FIGURES — CORE CATEGORIES

+----------------+------------------------------------+------------------------------+
| Category       | What is measured                   | Example (HS2 Phase 1)        |
+----------------+------------------------------------+------------------------------+
| Cost           | Final spend vs approved budget     | Actual vs £44.6bn baseline   |
| Time           | Actual completion vs planned date  | Days/weeks overrun or ahead  |
| Scope          | Deliverables completed vs agreed   | Km of track delivered        |
| Acceptance     | Client sign-off / handover status  | % sections accepted by DfT   |
+----------------+------------------------------------+------------------------------+
```

*Table 2: Completion figure categories with engineering example (NAO, 2023; OCR, 2017)*

> **Key OCR assessment point:** Completion figures must relate to the **end of the entire project**, not to individual tasks or stages during delivery. A status report produced mid-project is *monitoring data*, not a completion figure.

**Features:**
- Directly comparable to original project plan and baseline targets
- Expressed as absolute values (e.g., £2.1m over budget) or as percentages (e.g., 8% cost overrun)
- Collected through formal project closure documentation

**Benefits:**
- Provides clear, unambiguous evidence of whether planned targets were met
- Enables direct benchmarking against similar past projects
- Supports evidence-based decisions about future projects (Turner, 2014)

**Limitations:**
- Does not capture *why* variances occurred
- Gives no insight into stakeholder experience or satisfaction
- Can present an incomplete picture if quality is sacrificed to achieve cost/time targets

---

### 4.2 Issue Logs

An issue log is a **formal record of problems, risks, and obstacles** that arose during project delivery, together with the actions taken to resolve them. At project closure, the issue log becomes a valuable objective feedback tool because it provides a documented, factual account of challenges encountered throughout the project lifecycle (APM, 2019).

**Typical content of an issue log:**

```
ISSUE LOG — STRUCTURE

+--------+------------+-------------+---------------+------------+-----------+
| Ref No | Date Raised| Description | Impact (H/M/L)| Action     | Resolved? |
+--------+------------+-------------+---------------+------------+-----------+
| IS-001 | 14-Jan-26  | Steel supply | H             | Alt supplier| Yes       |
|        |            | delay 3 wks  |               | contracted  |           |
| IS-002 | 02-Feb-26  | Software bug | M             | Patch issued| Yes       |
|        |            | in SCADA sys |               | week 3     |           |
| IS-003 | 18-Mar-26  | Key engineer | H             | Contractor  | In prog.  |
|        |            | resigned     |               | engaged     |           |
+--------+------------+-------------+---------------+------------+-----------+
```

*Figure 2: Example issue log extract for a fictitious engineering installation project*

**Features:**
- Maintained throughout the project life — not just at closure
- Records facts: what happened, when, and what was done about it

**Benefits:**
- Provides a balanced account of the project, preventing a purely positive assessment
- Enables root cause analysis at closure
- Demonstrates accountability and due diligence to project sponsors and clients

**Limitations:**
- Only as reliable as the discipline with which it was maintained
- Does not capture stakeholder perceptions of how issues were handled
- Can be time-consuming to compile accurately throughout a long project

> **Why issue logs matter for success measurement:** Without consulting the issue log at closure, a project team risks drawing conclusions solely from positive completion data. The issue log ensures that what went wrong is documented alongside what went right, producing a more honest and balanced evaluation (Kerzner, 2017).

---

### 4.3 Complaints Analysis

Complaints analysis involves the **systematic examination of complaints received** during the project, typically from end users, clients, or affected third parties. Crucially, whilst individual complaints are inherently subjective (they express a person's dissatisfaction), the *analysis* of those complaints — classifying them by type, counting their frequency, and identifying patterns — produces **objective, measurable data** (OCR, 2017).

**Example from practice:**

During the Edinburgh Trams project, a significant number of public complaints were received relating to disruption along Princes Street. A complaints analysis would categorise these by issue type (noise, access disruption, construction dust), count their frequency over time, and identify which contractors or project phases generated the highest volume. This transforms individual subjective complaints into an objective dataset (Transport Scotland, 2011).

```
COMPLAINTS ANALYSIS — PROCESS

Individual          Categorisation     Frequency       Pattern
Complaints    -->   by Type       -->  Count      -->  Analysis
(subjective         (noise,            (objective       (What,
 opinions)          access,             data)            when,
                    quality etc.)                        who?)
```

*Figure 3: The complaints analysis process (adapted from OCR, 2017)*

**Benefits:**
- Converts qualitative dissatisfaction into quantitative evidence
- Highlights systemic problems that individual complaints might obscure
- Provides evidence for contractual or legal review if required

**Limitations:**
- Not all stakeholders will formally submit complaints; silent dissatisfaction is not captured
- Complaint volumes may reflect communication culture rather than actual performance levels

## 5. Subjective Feedback Methods

> **Time: 15 minutes | LO: LO6.2**

Subjective feedback methods capture opinions, attitudes, beliefs, and perceptions. The OCR specification (2017) classifies these as either **informal** or **formal**.

---

### 5.1 Informal Subjective Methods

Informal methods are **unstructured and spontaneous** ways of gathering stakeholder opinion. They are not designed in advance and do not follow a prescribed format. Whilst they may lack rigour, they can provide candid, unfiltered insight that more structured methods might not elicit.

#### a) Chat / Informal Conversation

Direct, unscripted conversations with team members, clients, or end users at project closure. For example, a project manager on the Elizabeth Line project might have informal post-handover conversations with station staff about their experience of the transition process.

**Benefits:**
- Generates candid, honest responses that people may withhold in formal settings
- Quick to conduct; no preparation required
- Can surface unexpected issues or positive outcomes

**Limitations:**
- Not systematic; coverage depends on who the project manager happens to speak with
- Cannot be easily quantified or compared across stakeholders
- Relies heavily on the project manager's ability to listen and interpret accurately

#### b) Media Coverage

The reporting of a project — particularly large infrastructure projects — in newspapers, industry journals, or online news sources provides an informal measure of public and professional perception. Coverage of the Crossrail/Elizabeth Line project in publications such as *New Civil Engineer* provides insight into how the engineering community perceived project delivery challenges and achievements.

**Benefits:**
- Provides an external, independent perspective
- Accessible and free to gather
- Captures public/professional reputational impact

**Limitations:**
- Media coverage may be sensationalised or incomplete
- Negative coverage often outweighs positive; not representative of all stakeholder views
- The project team has no control over what is reported or how it is framed

---

### 5.2 Formal Subjective Methods

Formal subjective methods are **planned and structured** approaches to gathering stakeholder opinion. They are designed in advance and applied consistently, which means they allow for comparison across respondents and over time.

#### a) Meetings

Formal end-of-project review meetings — often called post-project reviews or closure meetings — bring together key stakeholders to discuss performance. Unlike informal chat, these meetings follow a structured agenda and are minuted (APM, 2019).

**Key features:**
- Scheduled in advance with defined attendees (e.g., project sponsor, project manager, client representatives)
- Conducted with a prepared agenda; outputs recorded formally
- Often include presentations of completion data alongside discussion of experience

**Benefits:**
- Enables interactive discussion and clarification of points raised
- Allows diverse stakeholder views to be heard in one forum
- Minutes provide a documented record for future reference
- Particularly effective for gathering contextualised feedback from key decision-makers

**Limitations:**
- Can be dominated by senior voices; junior team members may withhold criticism
- Time-consuming and costly to organise, particularly on large projects with many stakeholders
- Interpersonal dynamics may inhibit candid feedback (e.g., reluctance to criticise a line manager's decisions)

#### b) Questionnaires / Surveys

Questionnaires are structured feedback tools containing pre-designed questions distributed to a defined group of stakeholders. They may be paper-based or digital and are widely used as satisfaction surveys at project closure (Saunders et al., 2019).

**Example questionnaire types in engineering projects:**
- Client satisfaction surveys (rating delivery against agreed criteria)
- Team member experience surveys (capturing views on team dynamics, leadership, and communication)
- End user acceptance surveys (assessing whether the delivered product meets operational needs)

```
QUESTIONNAIRE DESIGN — KEY CONSIDERATIONS

+---------------------+------------------------------------------------+
| Element             | Guidance                                       |
+---------------------+------------------------------------------------+
| Question type       | Mix of rating scales (1-5), yes/no, open text  |
| Scale clarity       | Define what each rating means (1=poor, 5=exc.) |
| Anonymity           | Consider anonymous responses for honest views  |
| Length              | Keep concise; too long reduces response rate   |
| Distribution method | Digital (faster) or paper (better for site     |
|                     | workers without regular computer access)       |
+---------------------+------------------------------------------------+
```

*Table 3: Questionnaire design considerations (adapted from Saunders et al., 2019)*

**Benefits:**
- Can reach a large number of stakeholders quickly and cost-effectively
- Anonymity encourages more honest responses, particularly regarding sensitive topics
- Responses can be quantified and analysed to identify trends across the stakeholder group
- Digital tools (e.g., Microsoft Forms, SurveyMonkey) make distribution and collation straightforward

**Limitations:**
- Cannot probe or clarify ambiguous responses as an interviewer can
- Low response rates can make findings unrepresentative
- Poorly designed questions can introduce bias or misinterpretation
- Respondents may rush through without providing meaningful answers

## 6. Selecting the Right Method: Features, Benefits & Limitations

> **Time: 8 minutes | LO: LO6.2**

In practice, engineering projects use a **combination** of objective and subjective feedback methods to build a complete picture of project success. No single method is sufficient on its own; each has distinct strengths and weaknesses that make it more or less appropriate depending on the context.

### 6.1 Comparative Overview

```
FEEDBACK METHODS — COMPARATIVE SUMMARY

+---------------------+------------+-----------+---------------------------+-----------------------------+
| Method              | Type       | Structure | Key Benefit               | Key Limitation              |
+---------------------+------------+-----------+---------------------------+-----------------------------+
| Completion figures  | Objective  | Formal    | Clear factual evidence    | No explanation of variance  |
| Issue logs          | Objective  | Formal    | Balanced view of problems | Only as good as maintenance |
| Complaints analysis | Objective  | Formal    | Patterns from volume data | Silent dissatisfaction lost |
| Chat                | Subjective | Informal  | Candid, unfiltered views  | Not systematic              |
| Media coverage      | Subjective | Informal  | External perspective      | Potentially sensationalised |
| Meetings            | Subjective | Formal    | Interactive discussion    | Dominated by senior voices  |
| Questionnaires      | Subjective | Formal    | Wide reach, anonymity     | No probing; low response    |
+---------------------+------------+-----------+---------------------------+-----------------------------+
```

*Table 5: Comparative summary of all feedback methods (OCR, 2017; APM, 2019; Kerzner, 2017)*

### 6.2 Context Matters: Choosing Wisely

The selection of feedback method should be guided by three factors:

1. **The stakeholder group** — a site workforce may be more candidly reached through informal chat or anonymous questionnaire than a formal meeting with senior management present.
2. **The nature of the project** — a major public infrastructure project (e.g., Thames Tideway Tunnel) will require media monitoring as part of its reputational assessment; an internal factory upgrade at Rolls-Royce may not.
3. **The available resources** — large formal meetings and comprehensive questionnaire exercises require time and cost; smaller projects may rely more heavily on informal methods.

> **Examiner guidance:** When answering exam questions on feedback methods, learners must go beyond simply naming the method. They are expected to explain its features, apply it to the given scenario, and critically evaluate its advantages and limitations within that specific context (OCR, 2017).

### 6.3 Worked Example

> **Scenario:** A civil engineering firm has completed a two-year bridge replacement project for a local authority client. The project manager wishes to evaluate project success at closure.

The following combination of methods would provide a comprehensive evaluation:

- **Completion figures** — compare final cost and time against the original contract; assess scope delivery against the bill of quantities.
- **Issue log review** — examine all recorded problems (e.g., unexpected ground conditions, material delays) to understand what went wrong and how it was managed.
- **Client satisfaction questionnaire** — distributed to the local authority's project representatives to capture their experience of the team's communication, responsiveness, and quality of delivered work.
- **Formal closure meeting** — held with the client and key subcontractors to discuss completion, transfer of documentation, and any outstanding concerns.
- **Informal conversation** — post-handover walkthrough with the local authority's maintenance team to gauge their satisfaction with the completed structure.

This blended approach ensures that both *what was achieved* (objective) and *how the project was experienced* (subjective) are captured (APM, 2019; Turner, 2014).

## 7. Conclusion & Recap

> **Time: 5 minutes | LO: —**

This lecture has examined how engineering projects measure success through the structured collection of feedback at project closure. The key learning from this session is summarised below.

### Key Points

- The **success of a project** is measured by judging how well its aims and objectives have been met, drawing on feedback from a range of stakeholders (OCR, 2017; APM, 2019).
- **Objective feedback** is based on measurable facts and encompasses three methods: completion figures, issue logs, and complaints analysis.
- **Completion figures** relate specifically to the *end of the entire project* — they are not mid-project monitoring data.
- **Complaints analysis** is objective because it analyses the *quantity and classification* of complaints, not the opinion within any individual complaint.
- **Subjective feedback** is based on opinion, attitude, and belief. It is gathered informally (chat, media coverage) or formally (meetings, questionnaires).
- Each method has distinct **features, benefits, and limitations**; the most effective approach combines objective and subjective methods to build a complete evaluation.
- In an exam response, simply naming a method is insufficient: learners must **explain its features** and **evaluate its advantages and limitations** in context.

### Self-Check Questions

Consider the following before the next session:

1. A project sponsor states that a project was "definitely a success" because it came in under budget. What type of feedback is this? What other feedback might be needed to form a balanced judgement?
2. During a project on the Elizabeth Line, 47 formal complaints were received relating to noise pollution. Explain why the analysis of these complaints is classified as objective feedback, even though the original complaints expressed subjective dissatisfaction.
3. A project manager chooses to collect feedback using only informal chat with team members. Identify two limitations of this approach.
