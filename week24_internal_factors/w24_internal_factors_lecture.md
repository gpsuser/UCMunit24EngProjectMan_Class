## 1. Objectives

> **Time allocation: 2 minutes | LO5.2**

By the end of this lecture, learners will be able to:

1. **Identify** the categories of internal factors that can affect the delivery of an engineering project.
2. **Explain** how organisational aims, procedures, scale, and scope create constraints on project outcomes.
3. **Describe** how resource constraints (physical, technological, and human) contribute to project deviation.
4. **Analyse** the consequences of poor leadership and poor planning on time, cost, and quality.
5. **Discuss** the role of ethics and corporate social responsibility (CSR) in shaping project decisions.

## 2. Introduction: Why Plans Fail

> **Time allocation: 5 minutes | LO5.2**

Even the most thoroughly prepared engineering project plan does not guarantee success. The Heathrow Terminal 5 baggage handling system collapse on opening day in 2008 — despite years of preparation — illustrates that internal organisational and operational factors can unravel even well-resourced projects (Hall, 2009). More recently, the HS2 Phase 1 project has experienced cost escalation from an original estimate of £37.5 billion to projections exceeding £67 billion, driven substantially by internal factors including scope changes, workforce capability gaps, and programme management weaknesses (National Audit Office, 2023).

The OCR unit specification draws a clear distinction between **internal** and **external** factors affecting project outcomes. This lecture is concerned with the internal category — those factors that originate **within** the delivering organisation and are, in principle, within its power to control or mitigate.

The diagram below illustrates where internal factors sit within the broader monitoring framework:

```
+--------------------------------------------------+
|           WHY PROJECTS ARE MONITORED             |
+-------------------------+------------------------+
|     INTERNAL FACTORS    |    EXTERNAL FACTORS    |
|  (within organisation)  |  (outside organisation)|
+-------------------------+------------------------+
| - Organisational aims   | - External stakeholders|
| - Procedures/policies   | - Market conditions    |
| - Scale and scope       | - Competition          |
| - Resource constraints  | - PESTLE factors       |
| - Poor leadership       |                        |
| - Poor planning         |                        |
+-------------------------+------------------------+
```

> **Key principle:** Continuous monitoring allows a project manager to detect the effects of internal factors early and take corrective action before minor deviations become costly failures (Turner, 2014).

## 3. Organisational Aims, Objectives, and Procedures

> **Time allocation: 8 minutes | LO5.2**

### 3.1 Organisational Aims and Objectives

A project does not exist in isolation. It is commissioned within an organisation that has its own strategic aims, operational priorities, and, frequently, competing demands on resources. When organisational priorities shift mid-project — for example, following a change in leadership, a merger, or a budget review — the project may find itself with reduced funding, a redirected scope, or outright cancellation.

**Example:** During the development of Rolls-Royce's Trent XWB engine programme, the company's strategic objective of securing a long-term position on the Airbus A350 platform meant that the project team was required to accept aggressive delivery timelines that internal engineering teams had flagged as high-risk (Rolls-Royce plc, 2014). The organisational aim of securing the contract overrode the technical team's schedule concerns, creating internal tension that project monitoring had to manage throughout delivery.

The implication for project managers is clear: **project objectives must be aligned with, and continuously reconciled against, the broader strategic aims of the organisation**.

### 3.2 Organisational Procedures and Policies

Every organisation operates according to established procedures — procurement rules, approval workflows, health and safety requirements, quality assurance protocols, and change control processes. These procedures exist for legitimate reasons, but they can also create constraints on project delivery:

```
COMMON PROCEDURE-DRIVEN CONSTRAINTS
+-------------------+------------------------------------------+
| Procedure         | Potential Impact on Project              |
+-------------------+------------------------------------------+
| Procurement rules | Multiple approval stages slow purchasing |
| Change control    | Formal sign-off delays scope amendments  |
| H&S compliance    | Work halted pending risk assessment      |
| IT security       | New software deployment restricted       |
| HR policies       | Redeployment of skilled staff limited    |
+-------------------+------------------------------------------+
```

**Implication:** A project manager must understand the organisation's procedural landscape from the outset and build realistic timescales that account for internal approval processes.

### 3.3 Ethics and CSR (introduced here, developed in Section 7)

Organisational policies increasingly incorporate ethical commitments and corporate social responsibility (CSR) obligations. These can affect project scope, material selection, and supply chain decisions. This topic is developed further in Section 7.

## 4. Scale, Scope, and Resource Constraints

> **Time allocation: 12 minutes | LO5.2**

### 4.1 Scale and Scope of the Project

The **scale** of a project refers to its overall size in terms of budget, workforce, and duration. The **scope** defines what is and is not included in the project deliverables. Both are internal factors because they are defined by the organisation commissioning the project.

Scope creep — the gradual, uncontrolled expansion of what a project is expected to deliver without a corresponding increase in time or budget — is one of the most common causes of project overrun. The Project Management Institute (2021) identifies scope creep as a contributing factor in 52% of project failures globally.

**Example — Crossrail / Elizabeth Line:** The original scope of the Crossrail project was substantially enlarged between 2007 and 2018 to include additional stations, accessibility upgrades, and revised fit-out specifications. These internal scope changes, agreed by the sponsoring organisations, contributed significantly to the project's £4 billion cost overrun and 3.5-year delay (National Audit Office, 2019).

```
SCOPE CREEP EFFECT ON THE TRIPLE CONSTRAINT

           TIME <-----------+
            |               |
            |   SCOPE       |
            |   CREEP    +--+------+
            |            |         |
            +----------->+ QUALITY +
                         |         |
            COST <-------+         |
               |         +---------+
               |
    Budget runs over; schedule slips;
    quality compromised without monitoring
```

### 4.2 Resource Constraints

The OCR specification identifies three categories of resource constraint, each of which can independently — or cumulatively — affect project delivery.

#### (a) Physical Resources

Physical resources include premises, tools, equipment, facilities, materials, and consumables.

```
+-------------------+----------------------------------------------+
| Resource Type     | Example Constraint                           |
+-------------------+----------------------------------------------+
| Premises          | Laboratory booked by another project team    |
| Equipment         | CNC machine unavailable due to maintenance   |
| Materials         | Steel supply delayed by internal procurement |
| Consumables       | PPE stock depleted; work halted              |
+-------------------+----------------------------------------------+
```

**Consequence:** Physical resource unavailability causes activity delays, which, if those activities are on the critical path, extend the overall project duration.

#### (b) Technological Resources

Technological resources include computer hardware, software, networks, and advancing technologies.

Engineering projects increasingly depend on digital tools — CAD/CAM systems, BIM platforms, project scheduling software, and simulation environments. Internal IT constraints can have significant project consequences:

- **Incompatible software versions** across teams create data exchange problems.
- **Network bandwidth limitations** slow collaborative work on large datasets.
- **Failure to adopt advancing technology** may render deliverables obsolete before completion.

**Example:** During the early phases of HS2's detailed design, multiple engineering consultancies used differing versions of BIM software, creating interoperability problems that required additional internal coordination effort (HS2 Ltd, 2022).

#### (c) Human Resources

Human resources represent the most complex and most frequently cited category of internal resource constraint. The specification identifies three distinct aspects:

```
HUMAN RESOURCE CONSTRAINT CATEGORIES
+-------------------------------+-------------------------------------+
| Aspect                        | How It Affects the Project          |
+-------------------------------+-------------------------------------+
| Support of senior management  | Lack of sponsorship = reduced       |
|                               | authority, funding & priority       |
+-------------------------------+-------------------------------------+
| Skills of the project manager | Inadequate PM → poor decisions,     |
|                               | missed risks, team disengagement    |
+-------------------------------+-------------------------------------+
| Training of team members      | Undertrained staff → rework, delays,|
|                               | quality failures, safety incidents  |
+-------------------------------+-------------------------------------+
```

**Staff turnover** is a particularly disruptive human resource constraint. When experienced engineers leave mid-project, the incoming replacements require time to reach equivalent productivity. Knowledge transfer is rarely complete, and institutional memory of project decisions is lost (APM, 2019).

**Example — Thames Tideway Tunnel:** Tierney et al. (2019) note that the complexity of the Tideway project required specialist tunnelling expertise that the UK labour market could not consistently supply, forcing the consortium to manage ongoing skills gaps through intensive subcontracting — an internal resource constraint that added both cost and coordination overhead.

## 5. Poor Leadership

> **Time allocation: 10 minutes | LO5.2**

### 5.1 Defining Poor Leadership in a Project Context

The OCR specification is explicit that poor leadership is an internal factor affecting project outcomes, and identifies three manifestations:

1. **Poor team management** — inability to direct, motivate, and develop the project team.
2. **Limited reporting** — failure to collect, collate, and communicate progress information adequately.
3. **Disregarding stakeholders** — neglecting the interests, concerns, and inputs of those affected by the project.

Leadership failure does not occur in isolation. It interacts with other internal factors: a poorly managed team is less able to cope with resource constraints; limited reporting means that scope creep goes undetected; disregarding stakeholders risks loss of support at precisely the moment when internal political backing is most needed.

### 5.2 Consequences of Poor Leadership

The following table maps leadership failures to observable project outcomes — the type of cause-effect linkage that OCR examination questions require learners to articulate:

```
LEADERSHIP FAILURE → PROJECT OUTCOME LINKAGES
+----------------------------+--------------------------------+
| Leadership Failure         | Likely Project Outcome         |
+----------------------------+--------------------------------+
| Failure to delegate        | PM becomes a bottleneck;       |
|                            | decisions delayed              |
+----------------------------+--------------------------------+
| Poor communication         | Team unaware of priorities;    |
|                            | duplicated or missed tasks     |
+----------------------------+--------------------------------+
| Failure to motivate        | Reduced effort and engagement; |
|                            | activities completed slowly    |
+----------------------------+--------------------------------+
| Limited reporting          | Variances undetected until     |
|                            | problems become critical       |
+----------------------------+--------------------------------+
| Disregarding stakeholders  | Loss of senior support;        |
|                            | late-stage requirement changes |
+----------------------------+--------------------------------+
| Poor conflict management   | Team dysfunction; absenteeism; |
|                            | staff turnover                 |
+----------------------------+--------------------------------+
```

### 5.3 Worked Example — HS2 Phase 1 Programme Leadership

The National Audit Office (2023, p.8) noted that HS2 Ltd experienced significant turnover in its senior leadership during the critical detailed design phase, with five chief executives in seven years. The report linked this leadership instability to inconsistent strategic direction, repeated scope rebaselining, and an organisational culture in which problems were not reported upward in a timely manner — a direct example of the "limited reporting" failure identified in the specification.

The consequence was that schedule slippage and cost escalation were identified later than they should have been, reducing the window for effective corrective action.

### 5.4 The Link Between Leadership and Time Creep

**Time creep** — the gradual slippage of a project schedule beyond its planned completion date — is a recognised consequence of poor leadership. When a project manager fails to inspire the team, allocate tasks clearly, or escalate emerging problems, activities take longer than planned, float time is consumed, and eventually critical path activities overrun (Kerzner, 2017).

> **Exam technique note:** When explaining how poor leadership causes time creep, always provide a **linked cause-effect chain**: e.g., "The project manager failed to resolve a conflict between two team members [cause], which meant that the electrical installation team worked less effectively together [intermediate effect], resulting in the installation phase taking three weeks longer than planned [project outcome]."

## 6. Poor Planning

> **Time allocation: 10 minutes | LO5.2**

### 6.1 What Constitutes Poor Planning?

The OCR specification identifies three specific planning failures, each of which represents a distinct internal risk:

```
POOR PLANNING — SPECIFICATION CATEGORIES
+--------------------------------+-------------------------------------------+
| Planning Failure               | Definition                                |
+--------------------------------+-------------------------------------------+
| Not updating schedules         | Failing to revise the Gantt chart / CPA   |
|                                | diagram when actual progress deviates     |
|                                | from plan                                 |
+--------------------------------+-------------------------------------------+
| Lack of coordination           | Activities not sequenced or linked        |
| of activities                  | correctly; dependencies overlooked        |
+--------------------------------+-------------------------------------------+
| No contingencies               | No buffer time, budget reserve, or        |
|                                | alternative resource arrangements         |
|                                | for foreseeable risks                     |
+--------------------------------+-------------------------------------------+
```

### 6.2 Not Updating Schedules

A project schedule — whether a Gantt chart or a CPA network diagram — is only useful as a management tool if it is **kept current**. When actual progress data is not regularly fed back into the schedule, the document becomes a historical artefact rather than a live control instrument.

The consequence of an outdated schedule is that the project manager cannot accurately assess whether current activities are on track, cannot identify emerging float time consumption on the critical path, and cannot make informed resource reallocation decisions (Turner, 2014).

**Key monitoring practice:** Schedules should be updated at agreed reporting intervals — typically weekly or fortnightly on most engineering projects — and the updated version circulated to the project board.

### 6.3 Lack of Coordination of Activities

Engineering projects involve multiple workstreams that must be correctly sequenced and whose interdependencies must be actively managed. Poor coordination manifests as:

- Activities starting before their predecessors have been completed.
- Parallel workstreams progressing at different rates, creating bottlenecks at integration points.
- Sub-contractors or specialist teams arriving on site before the conditions for their work are ready.

**Example:** On a manufacturing facility upgrade project, if the electrical installation team begins cabling before the civil engineering team has completed the duct work, rework is inevitable. This is a coordination failure — an internal planning problem, not an external one.

### 6.4 No Contingencies

A contingency is a pre-planned response to a foreseeable risk. Contingency planning is covered in LO1.2, but its absence is relevant here as a **monitoring trigger**: if there are no contingencies and an activity overruns, the project manager has no buffer and the critical path is immediately threatened.

Contingency forms include:

```
CONTINGENCY TYPES IN ENGINEERING PROJECTS
+-------------------+---------------------------------------------------+
| Type              | Description                                       |
+-------------------+---------------------------------------------------+
| Time contingency  | Buffer activity added to the schedule after       |
|                   | key milestone (e.g., 2-week commissioning buffer) |
+-------------------+---------------------------------------------------+
| Cost contingency  | Financial reserve (typically 10–15% of budget)    |
|                   | held for unplanned cost increases                 |
+-------------------+---------------------------------------------------+
| Resource reserve  | Agreed access to additional staff or equipment    |
|                   | in the event of key resource failure              |
+-------------------+---------------------------------------------------+
```

### 6.5 Consequences of Poor Planning

```
POOR PLANNING → PROJECT FAILURE CASCADE

  [Schedules not updated]
         |
         v
  [Deviations undetected]
         |
         v
  [Critical path activities slip]
         |
         v
  [No contingency to absorb slip]
         |
         v
  [Deadline missed / budget overrun]
         |
         v
  [Project failure or emergency escalation]
```

**Example — Wembley Stadium Reconstruction:** The reconstruction of Wembley National Stadium experienced delays related to coordination failures between the steelwork contractor (Cleveland Bridge UK Ltd) and the main contractor (Multiplex). Poor planning of the steel arch installation sequence, combined with the absence of adequate contingency provisions, contributed to a 14-month delay and significant cost overrun (Williams, 2008).

## 7. Ethics and Corporate Social Responsibility

> **Time allocation: 8 minutes | LO5.2**

### 7.1 Ethics in Project Decision-Making

Ethical considerations are internal factors because they reflect the values, policies, and commitments of the delivering organisation. Engineering projects routinely involve decisions with ethical dimensions:

- Which materials to specify (environmental impact, sourcing conditions).
- How to manage health and safety risks (legal minimum vs best practice).
- How to handle cost pressures (cutting corners vs maintaining quality standards).
- Transparency of reporting (accurate variance disclosure vs reputational management).

The Engineering Council's *UK Standard for Professional Engineering Competence* (Engineering Council, 2020) requires engineers to act with honesty and integrity, and to take account of the broader societal consequences of their work. When project managers encounter pressure from organisational leadership to suppress unfavourable progress data or reduce safety standards to meet budget targets, they face a direct conflict between ethical obligations and internal organisational pressures.

### 7.2 Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)

CSR refers to an organisation's commitment to operate in an economically, socially, and environmentally responsible manner. For engineering projects, CSR considerations translate into tangible project constraints:

```
CSR DIMENSION → PROJECT CONSTRAINT EXAMPLES
+-------------------------+--------------------------------------------+
| CSR Dimension           | Engineering Project Constraint             |
+-------------------------+--------------------------------------------+
| Environmental           | Use of low-carbon materials specified;     |
|                         | waste disposal protocols mandated          |
+-------------------------+--------------------------------------------+
| Social                  | Local labour sourcing commitments;         |
|                         | community engagement requirements          |
+-------------------------+--------------------------------------------+
| Governance/Ethical      | Anti-bribery compliance in procurement;    |
|                         | whistleblowing policy for contractors      |
+-------------------------+--------------------------------------------+
| Health & Safety         | Minimum standards exceed legal requirement;|
|                         | restricts working hours and methods        |
+-------------------------+--------------------------------------------+
```

### 7.3 When CSR Creates Project Constraints

CSR obligations are not always straightforward. They can create genuine internal tensions:

**Example — Crossrail Environmental Commitments:** The Crossrail project included a commitment to divert and reuse 100% of excavated material (approximately 7 million tonnes) rather than depositing it in landfill. This CSR-driven commitment was operationally complex and logistically demanding, adding coordination overhead and cost that a purely commercially focused procurement approach would not have incurred (Transport for London, 2019). Nevertheless, the commitment was internally mandated, making it an internal factor that the project team had to plan and monitor against.

### 7.4 Ethical Failures and Project Outcomes

Where an organisation's ethical culture is weak, internal pressure may manifest in project monitoring failure: problems are concealed rather than reported, scope changes are undisclosed, and the project board receives inaccurate information. Hattie and Timperley (2007) emphasise that feedback only improves outcomes when it is accurate and constructively received — a principle that applies to project monitoring reporting just as much as to educational feedback.

The internal cultural climate of an organisation is therefore itself an internal factor influencing whether monitoring is honest and effective, or performative and unreliable.

## 8. Conclusion and Exam Guidance

> **Time allocation: 5 minutes | LO5.2**

### 8.1 Summary

This lecture has established that the following categories of internal factor can affect the outcome of an engineering project, regardless of the quality of its initial planning:

```
INTERNAL FACTORS SUMMARY MAP

  +----------------------------------------------------------+
  |                  INTERNAL FACTORS (LO5.2)                |
  +------------------+---------------------------------------+
  | Organisational   | Strategic priority shifts; procedural |
  | Aims/Procedures  | constraints; policy compliance        |
  +------------------+---------------------------------------+
  | Scale & Scope    | Scope creep; undefined boundaries;    |
  |                  | mid-project rescoping                 |
  +------------------+---------------------------------------+
  | Resource         | Physical: equipment, materials        |
  | Constraints      | Technological: IT, software, networks |
  |                  | Human: skills, training, senior buy-in|
  +------------------+---------------------------------------+
  | Poor Leadership  | Team management; limited reporting;   |
  |                  | disregarding stakeholders             |
  +------------------+---------------------------------------+
  | Poor Planning    | Outdated schedules; uncoordinated     |
  |                  | activities; no contingencies          |
  +------------------+---------------------------------------+
  | Ethics & CSR     | CSR obligations; ethical conduct;     |
  |                  | cultural reporting climate            |
  +------------------+---------------------------------------+
```

The unifying principle is that **continuous monitoring is the mechanism by which these internal factors are detected and addressed**. A project that is not monitored cannot identify when internal constraints are beginning to cause deviation from plan, and corrective action is delayed until problems become critical (Turner, 2014).

### 8.2 Exam Guidance — Internal vs External

A common examination task requires learners to classify a list of factors as either **internal** or **external**. The following decision rule is helpful:

> **Internal factor:** the delivering organisation could, in principle, have foreseen, controlled, or mitigated this factor from within.

> **External factor:** the factor originates outside the organisation and is not directly within its control.

**Worked classification examples:**

```
+----------------------------------------------+----------+
| Factor                                       | Type     |
+----------------------------------------------+----------+
| Project manager lacks delegation skills      | Internal |
| Company procurement rules cause delays       | Internal |
| Skilled engineer resigns mid-project         | Internal |
| Company safety policy halts site work        | Internal |
| Steel price rises due to global inflation    | External |
| A key supplier goes into administration      | External |
+----------------------------------------------+----------+
```

> **Exam caution:** The contradictory response rule applies — if both 'internal' and 'external' boxes are ticked for a single item, zero marks are awarded for that item. Read each factor carefully before responding.

### 8.3 Key Terms for Revision

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Scope creep | Uncontrolled expansion of project scope without corresponding resource increase |
| Time creep | Gradual slippage of project completion beyond the planned schedule |
| Resource constraint | Limitation on the availability, quality, or cost of physical, technological, or human resources |
| Contingency | A pre-planned response to a foreseeable risk, providing a buffer against overrun |
| CSR | An organisation's voluntary commitment to responsible social, environmental, and ethical conduct |
| Poor leadership | Project management failure characterised by inadequate team management, limited reporting, and disregard of stakeholders |
