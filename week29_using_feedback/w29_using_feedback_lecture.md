## 1. Learning Objectives

*(3 minutes)*
*LO6.3*

By the end of this session, learners will be able to:

- Explain how end-of-project feedback is used to recognise successes and failures.
- Identify the strengths and weaknesses of a completed project using feedback data.
- Describe the process of conducting a lessons-learned review, including root cause analysis.
- Explain how project feedback drives business improvement — including training, documentation, and procedural change.
- Discuss the value of reviewing personal performance and monitoring stakeholder satisfaction at project closure.

> **Exam note:** LO6.3 carries up to 12 marks in a single question across the exam series. Extended-response questions at this level require contextual application — answers must be linked to the scenario provided, not stated in general terms alone (OCR, 2024).

## 2. Introduction: Why Feedback Matters at Closure

*(5 minutes)*
*LO6.3*

Every engineering project reaches a point of formal closure. At this stage, the practical work is complete — deliverables have been handed over, the team has dispersed, and the project manager produces a final report. However, closure is not simply the end of activity; it is also the beginning of organisational learning.

Feedback collected at the end of a project serves a fundamentally different purpose from the monitoring data gathered during execution. Monitoring data (earned value, variance reports, issue logs) supports decision-making *within* the project. End-of-project feedback, by contrast, supports improvement *beyond* the project — in future ventures, in organisational processes, and in the professional development of individuals.

The OCR Unit 24 specification (OCR, 2016) identifies six core uses of end-of-project feedback under LO6.3:

```
+----------------------------------------------+
|        USES OF END-OF-PROJECT FEEDBACK        |
+----------------------------------------------+
|  1. Recognise project successes & failures    |
|  2. Identify project strengths & weaknesses   |
|  3. Identify lessons learned                  |
|  4. Inform future projects                    |
|  5. Drive business improvement                |
|  6. Review personal performance               |
|     + Monitor stakeholder satisfaction        |
+----------------------------------------------+
```

These uses are interconnected. Recognising a failure, for example, is the starting point for identifying a lesson, which then informs a procedural improvement that benefits future projects. Understanding these connections — not just the individual items — is essential for higher-band examination responses (OCR, 2024).

**UK Engineering Context:** The Infrastructure and Projects Authority (IPA, 2023) reports that the UK public sector spends over £400 billion on projects and programmes. Yet post-project reviews are frequently omitted or conducted superficially, resulting in repeated errors across programmes. The IPA's *Government Major Projects Portfolio* data consistently show that schedule slippage and cost overruns recur partly because lessons learned are not systematically captured or applied.

## 3. Recognising Project Successes and Failures

*(10 minutes)*
*LO6.3*

### 3.1 The Balanced Evaluation Principle

A common error in project closure reviews is to focus exclusively on achievements — delivered on time, within budget, to specification. Whilst acknowledging success is important for team morale and stakeholder confidence, a review that omits failures produces a distorted picture (Kerzner, 2017). Feedback collected at closure must examine both sides of the outcome.

The OCR mark scheme (OCR, 2023) awards marks specifically for balanced evaluation: examiners expect students to explain that issue logs, for instance, should be reviewed *because* they prevent the success measurement from becoming skewed towards the positive. A project manager who only presents favourable metrics is not providing an accurate account.

### 3.2 What Counts as a Success or Failure?

```
+---------------------------+-----------------------------+
| PROJECT SUCCESSES         | PROJECT FAILURES            |
+---------------------------+-----------------------------+
| Delivered on time         | Schedule slippage           |
| Within approved budget    | Cost overrun                |
| Met quality standards     | Quality erosion             |
| Scope fully achieved      | Scope creep / scope gap     |
| Stakeholder acceptance    | Rejected deliverables       |
| Safety targets met        | Safety incidents            |
| Supplier performance met  | Contractor underperformance |
+---------------------------+-----------------------------+
```

**Important distinction:** Success and failure are measured against the *original* project objectives — the SMART targets agreed at the planning stage. If the scope was formally changed mid-project via change control, the revised objectives become the baseline for evaluation (Turner, 2014).

### 3.3 Example — Crossrail / Elizabeth Line

The Elizabeth Line, opened to the public in May 2022, serves as a detailed case study in mixed outcomes. The project was ultimately heralded as a significant engineering achievement — 42 km of new tunnels, 10 new stations, and a new east–west artery across London. However, the project was delivered approximately six years late and at a final cost of approximately £18.9 billion against an original estimate of £14.8 billion (National Audit Office, 2019). A balanced closure review acknowledges both the quality of the technical output and the significant schedule and cost failures, enabling the railway industry to learn from both.

## 4. Identifying Project Strengths and Weaknesses

*(8 minutes)*
*LO6.3*

### 4.1 Moving from Outcomes to Processes

Identifying a success or failure describes *what* happened; identifying a strength or weakness explains *why* it happened. This distinction is important in practice and in assessment. A project outcome is the result; a project strength or weakness is a characteristic of how the project was managed or resourced that contributed to that outcome.

```
OUTCOME                        STRENGTH / WEAKNESS
-----------------------------------------------------------
Delivered ahead of schedule  <-- Strength: Effective Gantt
                                 planning and resource levelling

Budget overrun by 12%        <-- Weakness: Risk register
                                 underestimated contingency

Stakeholders dissatisfied    <-- Weakness: Communication
with reporting frequency         strategy was inadequate
```

### 4.2 Sources of Evidence for Strength/Weakness Analysis

The feedback methods introduced in Week 28 (KPI data, completion figures, questionnaires, issue logs) provide the evidence base for this analysis. Key sources include:

- **Issue logs:** A formal record of problems encountered and their resolution. Reviewing the volume, type, and resolution time of issues reveals whether project management processes were robust.
- **Completion figures:** Final cost, time, and quality data compared against the approved baseline. Deviations indicate areas of weakness; compliance indicates strength.
- **Stakeholder satisfaction surveys:** Provide insight into perceptions of communication, responsiveness, and deliverable quality.
- **KPI dashboards:** Summary performance data across the project's critical metrics.

### 4.3 Presenting Strengths and Weaknesses: A Simple Framework

A structured approach, commonly used in post-project reviews, is to categorise findings by project management domain (APM, 2019):

```
+------------------+------------------------+------------------------+
| PM DOMAIN        | STRENGTH IDENTIFIED    | WEAKNESS IDENTIFIED    |
+------------------+------------------------+------------------------+
| Scope management | Clear WBS from outset  | Late scope changes     |
| Schedule         | CPA used effectively   | Float not maintained   |
| Cost             | Budget reviews weekly  | Contingency too low    |
| Quality          | Stage-gate reviews     | Final testing delayed  |
| Stakeholder      | Regular board updates  | End-user not engaged   |
| Risk             | Risk register active   | New risks not captured |
+------------------+------------------------+------------------------+
```

This domain-by-domain approach ensures that the review is systematic rather than impressionistic, and provides actionable findings rather than vague generalisations (APM, 2019).

## 5. Lessons Learned and Root Cause Analysis

*(12 minutes)*
*LO6.3*

### 5.1 What Is a Lessons-Learned Review?

A lessons-learned review is a structured process, conducted at or shortly after project closure, in which the project team — and, where appropriate, key stakeholders — collectively examine what happened, why it happened, and what should be done differently in future (PMI, 2021). It is the primary mechanism by which experience is converted into organisational knowledge.

The Association for Project Management (APM, 2019) defines a lesson as either a *positive lesson* (something that worked well and should be repeated) or a *negative lesson* (something that did not work and should be avoided or modified). Both types have equal value.

### 5.2 Root Cause Analysis

Identifying a problem is insufficient without understanding its origin. Root cause analysis (RCA) is a technique used to trace a failure or deviation back to its fundamental cause — rather than stopping at the surface-level symptom.

**The 5 Whys Technique**

One of the most widely used RCA methods is the *5 Whys*, originally developed within the Toyota Production System (Ohno, 1988). The principle is to ask "Why?" iteratively until the root cause is exposed.

*Example — Thames Tideway Tunnel (adapted):*

```
PROBLEM:   A tunnel boring machine segment was installed
           out of alignment, requiring costly rework.

Why 1:     The site supervisor approved the placement
           without checking the survey data.
Why 2:     The supervisor was under pressure to meet a
           daily progress target.
Why 3:     The daily target had been revised upwards
           without a risk assessment.
Why 4:     The schedule recovery plan was approved without
           consulting the engineering team.
Why 5:     The change control procedure did not require
           engineering sign-off for schedule changes.

ROOT CAUSE: Inadequate change control procedure.
ACTION:    Revise change control to require engineering
           approval for all schedule acceleration decisions.
```

This example illustrates how what appeared to be an installation error was, in fact, a process failure — and only a process-level corrective action will prevent recurrence (Saunders et al., 2019).

### 5.3 Conducting the Lessons-Learned Workshop

In practice, lessons-learned reviews are conducted as structured workshops. Best practice guidance (APM, 2019; PMI, 2021) recommends the following format:

```
LESSONS-LEARNED WORKSHOP STRUCTURE
+-------+----------------------------------+------------------+
| STAGE | ACTIVITY                         | TIME ALLOCATION  |
+-------+----------------------------------+------------------+
|   1   | Review project objectives &      | 10 minutes       |
|       | compare against outcomes         |                  |
+-------+----------------------------------+------------------+
|   2   | Team reflects on what went       | 20 minutes       |
|       | well (positive lessons)          |                  |
+-------+----------------------------------+------------------+
|   3   | Team reflects on what did not    | 20 minutes       |
|       | go well (negative lessons)       |                  |
+-------+----------------------------------+------------------+
|   4   | Root cause analysis for key      | 20 minutes       |
|       | failures                         |                  |
+-------+----------------------------------+------------------+
|   5   | Draft action recommendations     | 10 minutes       |
|       | and assign owners                |                  |
+-------+----------------------------------+------------------+
```

### 5.4 Documenting Lessons Learned

The lessons-learned register (also called a lessons-learned log) is the formal output of the review. Each entry should record:

- A clear description of the lesson (positive or negative)
- The root cause (for negative lessons)
- The recommended action
- The owner responsible for implementing the action
- The timeline for implementation

A lessons-learned register that is completed but never acted upon or shared is of little value. The document must be stored in a location accessible to future project managers, and its recommendations must feed into the organisation's standard procedures (Kerzner, 2017).

## 6. Informing Future Projects and Driving Business Improvement

*(10 minutes)*
*LO6.3*

### 6.1 From Individual Project to Organisational Capability

The value of a lessons-learned review is not confined to the team that conducted it. When properly managed, the knowledge generated flows outward into the organisation — improving training, updating procedures, refining risk registers, and strengthening the capability of future project managers (PMI, 2021).

This process is known as *organisational learning* — the mechanism by which an organisation becomes progressively more capable over time, rather than repeating the same mistakes on every project (Senge, 1990).

### 6.2 How Feedback Informs Future Projects

The table below summarises the direct links between lesson types and the project management artefacts they should update:

```
+---------------------------+--------------------------------------+
| LESSON TYPE               | ARTEFACT / AREA UPDATED              |
+---------------------------+--------------------------------------+
| Underestimated risk       | Risk register template updated       |
| Poor stakeholder comms    | Communication strategy revised       |
| Schedule overrun          | Planning templates and Gantt guidance|
| Contractor underperformance| Procurement and selection criteria   |
| Budget variance           | Cost estimation models revised       |
| Quality failures          | Quality plan checklists updated      |
| Safety incident           | Health & safety procedures revised   |
+---------------------------+--------------------------------------+
```

**Example — Heathrow Terminal 5:** The Terminal 5 programme, delivered by BAA and a consortium of contractors, introduced a ground-breaking *integrated project insurance* model that pooled risk between client and contractor, removing adversarial incentives. This model was itself a lesson learned from earlier Heathrow expansion projects where contractual disputes had driven cost and schedule overruns. The approach was subsequently adopted and recommended in the Egan Report (Egan, 1998) as a model for the UK construction industry, demonstrating how a single project's learning can inform sector-wide practice.

### 6.3 Driving Business Improvement

The OCR specification (OCR, 2016) identifies several specific mechanisms by which feedback drives business improvement:

**Training and expertise:** If the lessons-learned review reveals that the project team lacked a specific technical or managerial skill — for example, proficiency in earned value analysis — this creates a training need. The HR function can respond with targeted professional development.

**Procedures and documentation:** If a process was found to be inadequate — such as a change control procedure that did not require engineering sign-off — the procedure is formally revised and the updated version published to all project staff.

**Knowledge management:** Best practice documentation — templates, case studies, worked examples, model risk registers — is created or updated and stored in a central knowledge repository accessible to future project teams.

**Supplier and contractor relationships:** Feedback gathered from and about external parties (suppliers, contractors, consultants) can inform future procurement decisions, contract terms, and supplier management strategies.

### 6.4 The Continuous Improvement Cycle

The relationship between end-of-project feedback and future project performance can be represented as a continuous improvement cycle:

```
         +------------------+
         |  Project Closure  |
         +--------+---------+
                  |
                  v
         +------------------+
         | Feedback Gathered |
         | (LO6.1 & LO6.2)  |
         +--------+---------+
                  |
                  v
         +------------------+
         | Lessons Learned  |
         | Review (RCA)     |
         +--------+---------+
                  |
                  v
         +------------------+
         | Recommendations  |
         | & Best Practice  |
         | Documentation    |
         +--------+---------+
                  |
                  v
         +------------------+
         |  Future Project  |
         |  Planning &      |
         |  Execution       |
         +------------------+
                  |
                  +-----> (cycle repeats)
```

This cycle is consistent with the *Plan–Do–Check–Act* (PDCA) model associated with continuous quality improvement (Deming, 1986), widely applied in engineering and manufacturing organisations throughout the UK.

## 7. Reviewing Personal Performance and Monitoring Stakeholder Satisfaction

*(8 minutes)*
*LO6.3*

### 7.1 Reviewing Personal Performance

End-of-project feedback is not solely an organisational tool — it serves the professional development of individuals. The project manager, team leaders, and team members each benefit from reflecting on their personal contribution and identifying areas for development.

**Performance Review Methods:**

- **Self-assessment:** The individual reflects on their own performance against their role responsibilities and agreed objectives.
- **Line manager appraisal:** The project sponsor or functional manager provides a formal written assessment.
- **Peer review:** Colleagues who observed the individual's work provide structured feedback.
- **360-degree feedback:** A comprehensive method in which feedback is gathered from the individual's manager, peers, direct reports, and sometimes external stakeholders (APM, 2019).

The purpose of personal performance review at project closure is not merely to assign a grade. It is to identify specific development actions — additional training, mentoring, or stretch assignments — that will strengthen the individual's capability in future projects (Kerzner, 2017).

**Example:** A project engineer who managed a sub-team during the construction of a new manufacturing facility receives feedback that their technical decisions were sound but that their communication with non-engineering stakeholders was unclear. This generates a specific development action: completing a technical communication course before taking on a further management role.

### 7.2 Monitoring Stakeholder Satisfaction

Stakeholder satisfaction at project closure is an important measure of overall project success that complements the objective completion figures. A project can be delivered on time and within budget yet still leave key stakeholders dissatisfied — perhaps because their expectations were not properly managed, or because the final deliverable did not fully meet their operational needs.

Monitoring stakeholder satisfaction at closure involves:

- Reviewing responses from satisfaction surveys administered during and after project handover.
- Analysing complaints logs and issue resolution records.
- Assessing acceptance testing outcomes (did the client accept the deliverable on first submission?).
- Reviewing stakeholder feedback from project board meetings.

```
+--------------------+-------------------------------------------+
| STAKEHOLDER GROUP  | SATISFACTION INDICATORS                   |
+--------------------+-------------------------------------------+
| Client / Sponsor   | Formal acceptance; repeat business        |
| End users          | User acceptance testing; satisfaction     |
|                    | survey scores; complaint volume           |
| Suppliers          | Payment on time; relationship quality;   |
|                    | willingness to tender on future work      |
| Project board      | Confidence in PM; recommendation          |
| Regulatory bodies  | Compliance audit outcomes; sign-off       |
+--------------------+-------------------------------------------+
```

Dissatisfied stakeholders provide an important source of learning. If a client formally accepted a deliverable but later reported problems in operation, this gap between acceptance and satisfaction reveals a weakness in the acceptance criteria themselves — a lesson that should update the organisation's quality planning templates for future projects (PMI, 2021).

### 7.3 Exam Technique: Answering LO6.3 Questions

Examination questions on LO6.3 are frequently scenario-based and require contextual application. A common error is to state a use of feedback in general terms without linking it to the scenario. The mark schemes consistently award higher marks for contextual responses.

**Structure for a 2-mark LO6.3 response:**

```
POINT:   State the use of feedback clearly.
         e.g. "The feedback could be used to identify lessons
         learned."

EXPLAIN: Develop the point with a reason or mechanism.
         e.g. "By reviewing what went wrong with the
         procurement process, the organisation can update
         its supplier selection criteria to prevent similar
         delays on future projects."

CONTEXT: Link to the scenario provided.
         e.g. "In Anders plc's case, the delays caused by
         the Canadian supplier could inform a revised
         international procurement policy."
```

For 4-mark and extended-response questions, the same structure applies but must be applied to *multiple* uses with full development of each (OCR, 2024).

## 8. Conclusion and Exam Focus

*(4 minutes)*
*LO6.3*

### Key Points to Remember

This lecture has examined the six core uses of end-of-project feedback as defined by LO6.3 of the OCR Unit 24 specification:

```
+---+------------------------------------------+-----------------+
| # | USE OF FEEDBACK                          | KEY MECHANISM   |
+---+------------------------------------------+-----------------+
| 1 | Recognise successes and failures         | Compare against |
|   |                                          | original targets|
+---+------------------------------------------+-----------------+
| 2 | Identify strengths and weaknesses        | Domain-by-domain|
|   |                                          | analysis        |
+---+------------------------------------------+-----------------+
| 3 | Identify lessons learned                 | Lessons-learned |
|   |                                          | workshop + RCA  |
+---+------------------------------------------+-----------------+
| 4 | Inform future projects                   | Update planning |
|   |                                          | artefacts       |
+---+------------------------------------------+-----------------+
| 5 | Drive business improvement               | Training, procs,|
|   |                                          | knowledge mgmt  |
+---+------------------------------------------+-----------------+
| 6 | Review personal performance              | Appraisal,      |
|   |                                          | 360-degree      |
+---+------------------------------------------+-----------------+
|   | (+ Monitor stakeholder satisfaction)     | Surveys, KPIs,  |
|   |                                          | complaints data |
+---+------------------------------------------+-----------------+
```

### Critical Exam Distinctions

1. **LO6.3 is distinct from LO6.1 and LO6.2.** The question of *how* feedback is collected (methods) belongs to LO6.2. The question of *what that feedback is used for* (improvement and learning) belongs to LO6.3. Confusing the two is a common source of lost marks.

2. **Lessons learned require action to have value.** Stating that lessons were identified is insufficient — the lesson must be linked to a concrete improvement action (procedural update, training programme, template revision).

3. **Feedback benefits external stakeholders too.** In examination scenarios, students should consider that feedback serves not just the project organisation but also contractors, consultants, and suppliers who can use it to improve their own future performance (OCR, 2024).

4. **Balanced evaluation is always required.** Measuring success must include consideration of failures and weaknesses — a review that reports only positives is methodologically flawed and will not achieve high marks in extended-response questions.

### Summary Statement

The use of feedback at project closure is the final link in the project management cycle. It transforms experience into capability — for the organisation, for the project team, and for the individual engineer. An engineering organisation that systematically learns from its projects is progressively better at delivering them; one that does not is condemned to repeat the same errors at considerable cost to clients, stakeholders, and the public (Egan, 1998; IPA, 2023).
